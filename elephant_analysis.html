A close reading of paragraphs nine, ten, and eleven — power, humiliation, and the machinery of empire

9

Paragraph Nine

The Pressure of a
Watching Crowd

Summary

Orwell knows the correct action is to observe the elephant first, but he chooses not to. Even though he understands the risks, he feels pressured by the large crowd watching him. His fear of humiliation is stronger than his fear of danger, so he prepares to shoot the elephant.

👁

Point of View

First-person, internal and reflective. Orwell directly exposes his own psychological conflict — we are inside his mind as he debates and surrenders.

🎭

Themes

Social Pressure Fear of Humiliation Loss of Freedom Imperialism

Figures of Speech

Simile "like a toad under a steamroller" — Orwell feels crushed and powerless beneath the crowd's expectations, despite holding the gun.
Imagery "grinning corpse" — a grotesque image foreshadowing death and reducing a human body to a horrific object.
Hyperbole "two thousand Burmans" — the exaggerated crowd amplifies the suffocating weight of collective expectation.
Irony The man with the gun has no actual power — appearance of authority masks complete absence of free will.

"He wears a mask, and his face grows to fit it."

10

Paragraph Ten

Violence as
Entertainment

Summary

The crowd becomes silent and excited, treating the situation like a theatrical show. Orwell prepares to shoot and describes his rifle and aim with mechanical detachment, even though he remains deeply unsure. The moment has transformed from crisis into performance.

🔭

Point of View

First-person, descriptive and observational. The narrator zooms outward — noting the crowd — then inward, describing the rifle with cold, almost clinical precision.

🎭

Themes

Violence as Entertainment Public Expectation Dehumanization

Figures of Speech

Simile "as people who see the theater curtain go up" — the crowd's anticipation is equated to an audience awaiting a performance, stripping the act of any moral gravity.
Imagery The precise rifle description grounds us in physical reality, contrasting the narrator's inner chaos with outward mechanical control.
Personification The crowd moves and breathes as a single unified organism — a collective pressure that strips Orwell of individuality.
Symbolism The crowd symbolises society itself: watching, judging, demanding, consuming — never responsible for the outcome.

When the crowd expects a performance, even murder becomes theater.

11

Paragraph Eleven

The Slow, Agonising
Death

Summary

Orwell shoots the elephant, and the crowd cheers. The elephant does not die immediately but suffers and weakens slowly. Orwell fires several times until it finally collapses, delivering a prolonged and painful death that magnifies the moral horror of what has been done.

🎙

Point of View

First-person, dramatic and intense. The prose slows deliberately — each shot, each shudder of the elephant — forcing the reader to bear witness to the full weight of consequences.

🎭

Themes

Cruelty & Suffering Moral Guilt Consequences Imperialism

Figures of Speech

Imagery "immensely old," "legs sagging," "mouth slobbered" — visceral, physical imagery forces the reader to confront the brutality directly, without abstraction.
Simile "like a huge rock toppling" — the elephant's fall carries geological weight; something ancient and mighty is being destroyed.
Repetition "I fired…" repeated — the relentless repetition mirrors the drawn-out suffering, making the reader feel every shot alongside Orwell's deepening guilt.
Symbolism The elephant represents imperialism itself: enormous, once powerful, brought down slowly and painfully by forces that did not fully understand what they were destroying.

The tone is tragic and disturbing — Orwell condemns not just the act, but the entire system that made it inevitable.

The Imperialist Trap

Across all three paragraphs, Orwell reveals that the coloniser is not truly free. The system of empire enslaves both the colonised and the coloniser — each stripped of authentic choice.

The Arc of Guilt

The emotional arc moves from pressure (¶9) to performance (¶10) to punishment (¶11). Orwell is judge, executioner, and condemned — all simultaneously.

The Elephant's Role

The elephant shifts meaning across the three paragraphs — from burden (¶9), to prop (¶10), to symbol of empire's own destruction (¶11). Its death is allegorical.
