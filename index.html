Act One

The Pressure
of the Crowd

Burma, 1936. A colonial officer. A rifle. Two thousand watching eyes. And a choice that was never really a choice.

Scene Summary — Paragraph 9

Orwell knows the correct action is to observe the elephant first — but he cannot. The crowd's gaze is heavier than the rifle. His fear of humiliation outweighs his fear of death, and so he raises the gun.

Point of View

First-person, internal and reflective. We are trapped inside Orwell's mind as reason and instinct collapse under social pressure.

Themes

Social Pressure Humiliation Loss of Freedom Imperialism

Irony

The man holding the gun has zero power. Appearance of authority masks a complete absence of free will — the coloniser is also enslaved.

Simile"like a toad under a steamroller" — Orwell is crushed by expectation, powerless beneath the crowd despite holding the weapon.
Imagery"grinning corpse" — grotesque foreshadowing; death is already present before a single shot is fired.
Hyperbole"two thousand Burmans" — exaggeration amplifies the suffocating weight of collective expectation pressing on one man.
10

Act Two

Violence as
Theatre

The crowd goes silent. The curtain rises. What was once a crisis becomes a performance — and Orwell is the reluctant lead actor.

Scene Summary — Paragraph 10

The crowd falls into electric silence — not out of fear, but anticipation. They watch the way an audience watches a curtain rise. Orwell loads his rifle with mechanical detachment, unsure, yet utterly unable to stop.

Point of View

First-person, descriptive and observational. Orwell watches himself from the outside — almost as if he too is part of the audience.

Themes

Violence as Entertainment Public Expectation Dehumanization

Symbolism

The crowd symbolises society — watching, demanding, consuming — never responsible for what it forces others to do in its name.

Simile"as people who see the theater curtain go up" — transforms an execution into entertainment; strips the act of all moral gravity.
ImageryThe precise, cold rifle description contrasts Orwell's inner chaos — mechanical control masking human collapse.
PersonificationThe crowd breathes and moves as one organism — a single pressure that erases Orwell's individuality completely.
11

Act Three

The Long,
Agonising Fall

The crowd cheers. The elephant does not die. It suffers, slowly, shot by shot — and so does Orwell's conscience.

Scene Summary — Paragraph 11

Orwell fires. The crowd erupts. But the elephant refuses to die quickly — it staggers, sags, slobbers, and collapses in terrible slow motion. Each additional shot is a new moral failure. The prolonged death forces everyone — reader included — to bear witness.

Point of View

First-person, dramatic and intense. The prose slows deliberately — each shudder of the dying elephant stretches time, magnifying guilt.

Themes

Cruelty & Suffering Moral Guilt Consequences

Symbolism

The elephant is imperialism — enormous, ancient, brought down slowly and painfully by forces that never understood what they were destroying.

Imagery"immensely old," "legs sagging," "mouth slobbered" — visceral physical detail that refuses to let the reader look away from the brutality.
Simile"like a huge rock toppling" — the elephant's fall carries geological, ancient weight; something irreplaceable is being destroyed.
Repetition"I fired… I fired…" — relentless repetition mirrors the drawn-out suffering and deepens Orwell's accumulating guilt with every line.

The Full Picture

Three Acts. One Tragedy.
